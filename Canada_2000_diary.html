Note: the diary which follows relates true events, for the most part, but it is told by fictional characters from Motel California. These characters occasionally refer to a famous 70's rock musician in a in a familiar way. They also sometimes make reference to the former name of Motel California. As it is no longer safe to display either of these names on our website, they have been replaced by "[the Great One]" and "[Motel California]," respectively. In the one instance in which the name of the song after which the show used to be named appears, it is called "[the One True Song]."

September 17 , 2000

Vancouver, British Columbia
11:20 am

Ah, the Vancouver Fringe Festival is now over, and with it, this summer of theater in the great land of Canada. We would like to thank all the little people we met along the way, and all the big people as well. Rest assured that we will be returning next year.

Yours forever,
Gustave Flaubert


September 5 , 2000

Vancouver, British Columbia
10:40 pm

We have arrived safely in Vancouver, after another bus and ferry ride, to a house full of adults and children, with good music as well. Our new hosts Matt and Selina have just finished reading their daughters to sleep with a Harry Potter novel, and Selina is preparing for a camping trip tomorrow with some teenagers from the school where she works.

And how was Victoria, you ask? Well, they administer the death penalty for putting up posters on telephone poles, and of course for jaywalking as well, but there are a lot of nice people in Victoria, especially those involved with the Fringe, from our billets to the administration to all the volunteers. It was very well organized.

We played to an elementary school basketball court with 250 seats. We ourselves were on a raised auditorium stage. If we looked straight out from the stage, we could see the far basketball hoop at eye level, just beyond the last row of seats. Try as we might, we never could think of a good place in the show during which to shoot a basketball at that hoop. Ah well. We managed to fill the house to around 60-100, on average, which was enough to reduce a little bit of the cavernous feeling of the place.

In this particular Fringe, we also had a four-day break in the middle, during which we availed ourselves of the camping opportunities on the west side of Vancouver Island, in and around a town called Tofino, which reminded me of Stinson Beach, California, in the U.S.A. We were given a ride up with the gracious members of the cast of another Fringe show, called Etching. The first night in Tofino, all the good camping areas were full, and we had to stay at a place that was between the golf course and the airstrip. When we got there, our neighbors in the campsite next door were using a chainsaw to cut branches off the trees in their campsite, for firewood. When they finished, they built themselves a nice fire, produced a TV and a VCR and a mysterious power source, and proceeded to watch the movie "Fight Club." I have never seen "Fight Club," but I could tell it was "Fight Club" by a section of dialog which occurred early on in the movie, and went like this: "The first rule of Fight Club is that you can't leave. The second rule of Fight Club is that you have to fight." Or something.

The next day we went to a beautiful campsite on the beach and walked through the coastal rainforest, with lots of moss and ferns and tall trees. I hitchhiked back to town one day before the others. Out of the ten rides I got, three were with recovering heroin addicts. Those who have seen the dark side will always lend a helping hand.

Yours in the light of [the Great One],
Gustave Flaubert


August 22, 2000

Victoria, British Columbia
11:23 pm

Dear Diary,

I'm back. nhar. Gustave said I could write some more if I wanted, so here goes again. Well, we went through Prince George and still no word from [the Great One]. I tell you, it is getting tiring, diary. There was a funny smell in the air. Could this be a message? Gustave said no. Threre was a "pulp mill nearby." Of course, this sounded fishy to me. I was briefly reminded of the grapefruit from the last town, but no. hmmm. We performed in a Native American center and there was a giant dreamcatcher on the wall, so we left behind the dream of Brigitte the Turtle and Henry David Thoreau for it. It was a good catch of the day.

Then we went in a van with some guys who answer any question you can give them. But then their van broke down and nobody knew how to fix it. So we made pasta shells by the side of the road and caught some rays.

Now we're in Victoria. We had to take a boat here, a boat with a belly full of cars and busses and Winnebagoes, like the Nigels have. Victoria is very clean. Soon we will perform. Will [the Great One] be in the audience? If he is, will he have heard Blue Moon before? It's weird, but I think some of the people who have seen the show have heard that song before. somewhere. somehow. humph.

I'm sleepy, diary. Sometimes I miss New York and everyone in it. every one. Sometimes I miss Winnipeg. Sometimes Saskatoon. And Prince George, where there are many nice people, like Magdalena and Morris and Kenna, who let me stay in her house with her dog and cat and three birds.

Talk to ya soon.

nhar


August 14, 2000

Jasper, Alberta
12:54 am

Nhar and I met a fellow on the train to Jasper who lives in this town, and who told us the telephone number of a woman who rents out rooms. When we got to Jasper, I called her and said, "Hello, my name is Gustave. I met your friend Kevin on the train, and he said you rent out rooms. Would you have a room available for my friend and I for two days? We are weary after our long journey from Saskatoon."

"Yes," she said, "I rent out a room. I have to go to work in about twenty minutes, but if I'm gone when you get here, I'll leave the door to my house open for you." As I have said before, these Canadians are crafty. One must watch them at all times, to make sure they do not take advantage.

The place we came from yesterday, Saskatoon, is a smaller city than Winnipeg, with fewer mosquitoes. It is named after a local berry (actually, they have them in Winnipeg as well) which was particularly fancied by an early explorer. But I see that it has been so long since my last missive (almost a month! Good heavens) that I must give a brief synopsis of what has happened since then, without the natural history annotation.

We closed in our first Fringe city, Winnipeg, after selling out six out of eight shows. After a brief but delightful camping trip in a mosquito-laden but quite beautiful area northwest of Winnipeg, we proceeded by van to Saskatoon. The Saskatoon Fringe was quite wonderful, and quite a bit slower-paced. My only complaint was the lack of a covering over the beer tent. It rained a lot.

The second best thing to happen to us in Saskatoon was the Critic's Choice Media award, which granted us an extra performance the day after everyone else's shows ended. We shared this honor with two other shows, which got the Critic's Choice Audience award and the Critic's Choice Volunteer award. They selection process was all very democratic for the latter two awards; I assume ours was also, although you never know what those journalists are doing behind closed doors.

The best thing to happen to us by far, though, was the gift we got from our fellow performers. On the last night, hours before we were to catch our 1:20 am train for Jasper, we were presented with a card which had been signed by sixteen of our fellow travellers, who will be going to different Fringes than us for the next two runs or so, and whom we will sorely miss.

We look forward with great anticipation to our next Fringe in Prince George, BC, where our stay will be all too brief. We fervently hope that our five days in that town will be enough for the word of [the Great One] to sink in.

Yours on the road,

Gustave Flaubert


August 8, 2000

Saskatoon, Saskatchewan
11:05 am

Dear Diary,

It's me, nhar. Gustave told me I could, if I wanted, tell you, diary, about some of our trip. So here goes. First we went to Winnipeg. There we met Clytemnestra and Electra, two women from Seattle; a guy named [the Great One's First Name] from Denver, but his last name wasn't [the Great One's Last Name], it was Quixote; Hitler from England; and some worried clowns from nowhere in particular. In Winnipeg, during the song of killing, one of the owls got very bright and then went out. hmmm. Gustave brought me to stay with a nice man who used to work in a library. He made good food and even changed my sheets. No word from [the Great One].

When we were done performing there, Clytemnestra and Electra and a guy named Matt took us in a blue van to a slimy lake. There we saw very bright light in the sky, different than the moon and stars. Gustave told me this light was called the northern light. He had never seen it before either, and I thought maybe it was a message from [the Great One], but Gustave says no. I thought I heard it make a noise. But maybe it was just the loudness of the insects. hmmm. We sang Lola by the Kinks around a fire.

Then it was here to Saskatoon. It is a city surrounded by sunflowers. And spirals of hay. We are in a nice house with a nice family. one cat named "grizzly". One day, diary, I was walking through town and I heard a man with a guitar singing [the One True Song]. I stopped and listened. After it was over I checked to see if it was maybe [the Great One] in disguise. No, it wasn't. In Saskatoon we met two teenage pop stars named Nigel and Nigel, and their aunt. They drove around in a Winnebago from the 1980's. Also we met a mindreader named Louis and his assistant Greg. Gustave gave me a grapefruit to protect me from scurvy. hmmm. Still no word from [the Great One].

I'm gonna go, diary. Talk to ya later.

nhar

July 22, 2000

Winnipeg, Manitoba
10:33 am

It is I, Gustave Flaubert, on the road with Nhar. We are now well on our way across Canada, having opened [Motel California] last night, the second night of the Winnipeg Fringe Festival.

Our train journey went well. After leaving New York City on Amtrak's Maple Leaf Express the morning of Friday, July 14th, with a too-brief overnight stopover in the fair city of Toronto, we travelled for thirty hours in a VIA Rail Canada train (it's called the "Canada") built in the nineteen-fifties, with a restored art deco interior and plenty of friendly Canadians to keep us company, of both the English-speaking variety and the French-speaking variety. Over twenty-four of those thirty hours were spent in the province of Ontario. Ontario has many lakes, and also quite a few trees. I (Gustave) had never been there before. I don't know about Nhar.

Since arriving in Winnipeg, Manitoba, on the afternoon of Sunday, July 16th, we have been quite happy with the warm reception given by all, from our gracious billeting hosts Patrick Wright and Gary Montgomery to everyone at the Winnipeg Fringe Festival, and everyone in Winnipeg. Yesterday a woman we met on the street told us we absolutely had to visit the Historical Museum, then offered to pay for our tickets. Also yesterday, a food vendor sold me a slice of watermelon for the agreed upon price of "one loony" (the Canadian dollar coin has a loon on the back — the two-dollar coin is called a "toony") and then insisted that I take another slice. Countless other examples abound, but alas, space is limited in this diary.

They say it is very cold in the winter in Winnipeg, cold enough in fact to freeze exposed skin solid in one minute, but I think that this is not possible, unless you have stopped generating heat from the inside, say for instance if you have recently died of hypothermia. Likewise in the summer they say the mosquitoes blanket the earth, but we haven't seen many. You have to watch these Canadians. They are very friendly and helpful, but I think they are prone to exaggeration and trickery. I count my money when it comes out of the ATM machine, to make sure there is not too much.

And what about the show, you ask? How much ignorance of [the Great One] has been banished from the world?

After about two minutes of orienting ourselves last Sunday, we began to frantically poster the downtown Winnipeg area and to present everyone we met with a postcard, which we were told to call not a postcard but a flyer, which is apparently a more classy designation for our glossy publicity materials.

Our first performance was last night, Friday, July 21st at 11:00 pm. We had no idea what to expect and were feeling a little lonely without our special guests, but all our evangelism paid off in an opening night crowd of about seventy-five people, which is, to be quite honest, more than we have ever had anywhere outside of Prague.

This excellent turnout will help us in our mission to let the world know of [the Great One] for the rest of the Winnipeg Fringe Festival and for the other Fringes after that, but Nhar and I have resolved not to rest on our laurels. We will continue to spread the word, both inside our show and out, that more Canadians may come to know of [the Great One] and of his good works.

Goodbye for now,

Gustave Flaubert
